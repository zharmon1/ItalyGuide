The Dynamicity of Rome

Rome is an extremely diverse city in every way. From the people to the architecture to the age of everything around, Rome truly is a dynamic city. One of the best examples of this, in my opinion, is the walk from Hotel Trastevere to the Colosseum.

We begin our journey at Hotel Trastevere, a small hotel just next to Piazza San Cosimato in the Trastevere neighborhood of Rome. It is a very Italian area with few tourists compared to the rest of the city. During the day, the piazza is home to produce vendors who are selling fresh fruits and vegetables for their neighbors to use in their meals for the day. During the afternoon and night, the piazza is the local hangout spot for the Italian teens who all just got out of school. Around the area, there are many small local restaurants and convenience stores littered around the blocks.

The next large attraction of our walk is the Ponte Garibaldi bridge that crosses the Tiber River. Here is where we leave Trastevere and begin seeing more tourists. Build in the 1880s, the bridge is a beautiful feat of engineering that fits perfectly into the city, while still having its own personality. Unlike most other bridges around, here you find up to 4 lanes of busy traffic along with a full walkway on each side. The bridge acts not only as our bridge from Trastevere to the rest of Rome, but also as our bridge from the simpler, poorer, and more local areas to the eccentric, rich, and tourist-heavy heart of the city.

Soon after crossing the bridge, you will come across the small Piazza Benedetto Cairoli. This little gem is filled with people hanging out, playing with their dogs, and enjoying the greenery (which is a rarity in the city). The piazza contrasts very well with the cityscape as trees line the perimeter, almost allowing you to forget you are in a large city for just a few moments. Around this point of the journey is where the small convenience stores and local restaurants begin to become souvenir stores and tourist trap restaurants. The prices go up and the waiters urge you to come eat, and it only gets worse as you go on.

Next we get to see our first ancient Roman ruins at the Largo di Torre Argentina. This small area was home to Pompey’s Theater and is believed to be where Julius Caesar was killed. The sight of this small area with significant history that is surrounded by the more modern city built up around it is an amazing sight to see. All around you are cars and buses speeding by and honking with crowds of people going in and out of stores and restaurants, but in the center of it all is the small, seemingly innocent ancient square where one of the most famous betrayals in history took place.

The next large sight is also an insanely important sight for Italian history. It is the Altar of the Fatherland, a large monument that was built from 1885 to 1935 to honor the first king of the unified Italy, Victor Emmanuel II. Going from the sight of the death of a former Roman leader in 44 BC to the monument of the first king of the unified Italy in 1861, about 1900 years later. It is an amazing way to see for yourself how old and dynamic Rome truly is with its layered past.

Finally, you walk past the Roman forum and up to the Colosseum. All along the way, what were once local stops have become nothing but tourists traps, cheap souvenirs, and street vendors. Meanwhile, you’ve seen the true Italian lifestyle turn into modern life, into ancient history, into more recent history, and into ancient history again. However, don’t be fooled, surrounding the history in one area is always buildings or objects from another time. That can be seen not only at the location of Caesar’s death, but also at the Colosseum, which is surrounded by more modern homes, hotels, and restaurants. This walk truly is the best way to experience the dynamicness of Rome with its layered history. Rome really does have a story to tell in each turn.

Context

This was my work for the second project of the class. The assignment was to create a creative writing piece that shows the dynamism of Rome with its mix of ancient ruins and modern shops and architecture, beauty and decay, quiet and noisy spaces, wealth and poverty, stone and greenery, and everything else that is similar. I chose the walk from our hotel to the Colosseum because during this time each of the previously mentioned contrasts are on display. To add a bit of creative writing to the piece, I tried to write as some sort of tour guide who is pointing out all of these contrasting things as we are walking along.
